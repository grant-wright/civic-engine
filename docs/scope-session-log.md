## Where You Started

You arrived with a big, sprawling vision: a cooperative-but-personal board game crossing Monopoly, Civilization, SimCity, and Pandemic. A real simulated economy. Waterworks that need income. Streets being developed. A landlord who has to deal with a real estate agent, get council consent, secure a bank loan.

You said yourself: "maybe that's all too complicated" — but you shared it anyway. That was the right call.

## The Flipped Interaction

The early phase of this conversation was an interview — you being asked questions rather than prompting the AI. That's the "flipped interaction" pattern: instead of asking Claude to generate ideas, you generated the ideas and Claude drew them out.

The insight is that the quality of everything Claude produces downstream is only as good as the context you gave it upstream. The richer your brain dump, the better the scope doc, PRD, spec, and build.

## Key Learning Moments

**1. "Non-zero-sum" as a design principle**
You articulated this clearly: you didn't want a zero-sum game where one player's win is another's loss. You wanted a game where good collective decisions grow the pie — everyone can win more. Claude reflected it back as *"the tension comes from managing shared risk, and the reward is a genuinely bigger outcome for everyone."* You said: *"I love that sort of thing."* That became the game's philosophical core.

**2. Dunbar's Number — unprompted**
When talking about agent networks, you naturally landed on 150 as a cap — the natural limit of stable human social relationships (Dunbar's Number). You'd already internalized this concept. Claude noticed it and named it. The game's social capital mechanic is built on that insight.

**3. "Dilemmas between competing goods, not two evils"**
When the idea of internal councillor value contradictions came up — a bohemian-greenie who also values church and state — you named a design constraint: dilemmas should pit two good things against each other, never force a player to choose between two bad things. The game respects the player. That's a meaningful design principle, not just a preference.

**4. Abstain and defer as real options**
Real councillors can abstain or defer decisions. You wanted that modeled in the game — with real political consequences either way. That's civic realism, and it teaches something true about how governance works.

**5. The Steampunk pivot**
Asked what the city looked like in your head, you described post-earthquake Christchurch — empty lots, condemned buildings, the chaos of a city mid-rebuild. Then you consciously chose *not* to make the game about that. You reached for something more joyful: a bustling Victorian city of industry. Then "steampunk" clicked, and the whole aesthetic direction crystallized. Victorian cobblestones + smart city data dashboard overlay. The city's *look* evolving as players build. That's a mechanic, not just decoration.

**6. The faction reveal moment**
Claude suggested the setup moment — factions revealed like flipping cards to see what city you've inherited. Your response: *"wow yes, I love that!!"* Small design beat, but it transforms the opening of every game into a dramatic moment.

**7. Era transitions as the game's tension peaks**
Irish navvies losing work to the railway. Steam workers displaced by electrification. Capital sunk in canals when the future is rail. These aren't background flavor — they're the educational point of the game. Transitions are costly and people bear the cost. You arrived at that insight through the conversation.

**8. "Tradies non-billing while sitting gridlocked"**
The most human metric in the whole conversation. A real economic consequence that real people in Christchurch feel. That belongs in the quarterly report.

## How the Idea Was Scoped Down

**Full vision →** cooperative civic sim with full economy, Dunbar-scale agent networks, forking timelines, physical board, multiple domains.

**Key cuts made:**
- Physical board → central laptop screen (you suggested this)
- Full Dunbar networks → simplified faction groups for the demo
- Forking timeline → named future feature
- Multi-domain simulation → focused on transport only (you suggested this)
- Physical cards → all digital
- Multiple cities → one city, one campaign

**What survived:** The cooperative structure, the AI agents with inner lives, the non-zero-sum philosophy, the civic education purpose, the election survival mechanic, the prestige bid as long arc.

## The Process

1. Brain dump — everything out of your head, unfiltered
2. Research — three reference projects shown, your reactions shaped the direction
3. Gap sharpening — audience, win condition, Claude's role
4. Cutting — explicit decisions about what's out
5. V1 scope doc written mid-conversation
6. Deepening round — factions, councillors, dilemmas, aesthetic, era transitions
7. V2 scope doc written with substantially richer content

The difference between v1 and v2 is the value of the deepening round. V1 was competent. V2 has a soul.
